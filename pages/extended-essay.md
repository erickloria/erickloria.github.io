## 1. Purpose

The Physics EE is not a long lab report and not a history of physics. It is a 4,000-word, data-driven scientific investigation that:

- asks a focused, measurable research question grounded in Physics;
- uses accepted scientific methodology;
- produces analyzable data, whether primary or secondary;
- leads to a defensible, evidence-based conclusion.

> **Highlight**  
> A Physics EE is about doing Physics: modelling, measuring, analysing, and evaluating. It is not about biographies, timelines of discoveries, or opinion pieces about technology.

## 2. What Is a Physics EE?

In the IB sense, a science EE in Physics usually has these features:

- a clearly stated physical system or phenomenon;
- a research question that involves variables you can define and measure;
- a methodology based on accepted physics principles and standardized procedures;
- data, primary or secondary, that can be reliably analysed;
- a logical chain from theory to method to data to analysis to conclusion.

You may choose either of these pathways:

- **Primary data EE:** you design and carry out an experiment and collect your own measurements.
- **Secondary data EE:** you use published datasets, mission archives, physics databases, or research papers and perform your own analysis.

Both approaches are valid. What matters is how well you think with Physics, not who collected the raw numbers.

> **Best Practice**  
> Examiners look for ownership of the science: do you understand the models you use, the limitations of your apparatus or dataset, and the meaning of your graphs, fits, and uncertainties?

## 3. Recommended Structure and Sections

The structure below is the expected draft structure. Headings can be adjusted slightly, but the logical flow should remain intact.

### 3.1 Title Page

Include:

- full title of the investigation;
- clearly stated research question;
- subject: Physics;
- word count for the main body only.

Avoid vague titles. Strong titles are operational.

- **Weak:** Parachutes and Falling Things
- **Strong:** How does canopy diameter affect the terminal velocity of a 50 g model parachute in still air?

### 3.2 Table of Contents

- Include all sections and subsections with page numbers.
- Make sure the headings in the body match the labels in the contents.

### 3.3 Introduction (approx. 250-450 words)

Explain:

- what physical phenomenon you are studying;
- which core physics ideas are involved;
- why the topic matters, personally and/or scientifically;
- why the chosen method or data source is appropriate.

End the introduction with:

- a clear aim of the essay;
- the exact research question.

> **Best Practice**  
> Think of the introduction as a briefing to another physicist. After reading it, they should know what you are studying, why it is interesting, and roughly how you will investigate it.

### 3.4 Research Question

State the research question precisely and operationally.

Examples:

- How does the diameter of a copper wire affect its electrical resistance at constant temperature?
- How does the angle of release of a simple pendulum affect its period for angles between 5 degrees and 40 degrees?

Immediately after the research question, specify briefly:

- the independent variable and its range, with units;
- the dependent variable and how it will be measured;
- the controlled variables and how they will be kept constant.

### 3.5 Background / Theory (approx. 800-1200 words)

This section grounds the EE in Physics. Include:

- clear definitions of key terms and quantities;
- relevant laws, models, and equations;
- assumptions and limitations of those models;
- diagrams that illustrate the system and are properly labelled.

Every paragraph in the theory section should help you interpret the data later.

> **Highlight**  
> If a paragraph of theory does not help you understand or explain your data later, it probably does not belong in the EE.

### 3.6 Variables

Create a clear subsection that lists:

- the independent variable, including name, units, chosen range, and justification for that range;
- the dependent variable, including how it will be measured and with what instrument;
- the controlled variables, including how each could affect the outcome and how each will be controlled or monitored.

### 3.7 Equipment and Materials

- List all apparatus with relevant details.
- Include instrument uncertainties where appropriate.
- Add a labelled setup diagram or photo when useful.

### 3.8 Methodology

This is the recipe for the investigation. It must be detailed enough that another student could replicate it.

For primary data essays, include:

- a step-by-step procedure;
- the number of trials and why that is sufficient;
- calibration procedures or preliminary tests;
- safety, ethical, and environmental considerations where relevant.

For secondary data essays, include:

- how datasets or papers were selected;
- why the sources are compatible and comparable;
- how units, time frames, or other conventions were standardized.

### 3.9 Results and Data Processing

#### Raw data

- Present raw measurements in tables with clear headings and units.
- Keep significant figures consistent with instrument resolution.
- Show repeated trials clearly and indicate how you will average them if needed.

#### Processed data

- Show sample calculations for each derived quantity.
- Propagate uncertainties for key quantities.
- Present processed values in a separate table when helpful.
- Include graphs with clear titles, labelled axes with units, and appropriate fits or trendlines.
- If used, comment on `R^2` or another measure of goodness of fit.

> **Best Practice**  
> A strong Physics EE often has fewer but better graphs: clear, readable, and tightly linked to both theory and the research question.

### 3.10 Analysis

This is where the data is interpreted using Physics.

- Describe the trends and link them directly to theoretical models.
- Compare slopes, intercepts, or fitted constants with predicted or accepted values.
- Discuss the size and impact of uncertainties.
- Distinguish between random and systematic error.

### 3.11 Discussion and Evaluation

Step back and critically examine the investigation.

- Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of the methodology.
- Identify the most significant limitations.
- Discuss anomalies and plausible explanations.
- Suggest realistic improvements and possible extensions.

### 3.12 Conclusion

A strong conclusion:

- restates the research question and answers it clearly;
- refers explicitly to the key numerical results and trends;
- acknowledges the role of uncertainties and limitations;
- does not introduce new data, models, or ideas.

### 3.13 Reflection (for the Viva Voce and Criterion E)

Throughout the process, you should be able to discuss:

- how the research question evolved;
- how setbacks or unexpected results changed your approach;
- what you learned about how Physics is actually done.

### 3.14 Bibliography and Appendices

- Use one citation style consistently.
- Prioritize strong physics sources such as textbooks, journal articles, technical documentation, and reputable datasets.
- Use appendices for long tables, raw data, or calibration details, not for essential analysis.

## 5. IB Physics EE Criteria (A-E) Overview

The Extended Essay is assessed using five criteria. The summaries below are written for students and adapted to the Physics context.

### 5.1 Criterion A: Focus and Method

**IB focus (sciences):**  
The research question should drive the method, and the method should produce scientific data that supports a logical response.

**What this means for you:**  
Your research question must be specific, measurable, and clearly about Physics. The method must produce data that directly addresses that question.

**Best-practice checklist**

- The research question contains a clear independent variable and dependent variable when appropriate.
- The topic is feasible with available time, equipment, or accessible secondary data.
- The method is replicable.
- You understand and own the procedure rather than merely copying it.
- Data tables, diagrams, and graphs are labelled clearly, and key sample calculations include uncertainties.

> **Rubric Link**  
> A focused question supports stronger data analysis and stronger critical evaluation. A vague question weakens the entire essay.

### 5.2 Criterion B: Knowledge and Understanding

**IB focus (sciences):**  
You must demonstrate knowledge and understanding by exploring the relevant processes and theories with clear terminology.

**What this means for you:**  
Your essay should show that you understand the Physics behind the topic. Laws, principles, and equations should be explained accurately and applied to your specific investigation.

**Best-practice checklist**

- The theory section is tightly linked to the research question and method.
- All equations are defined clearly and symbols are explained.
- Assumptions and approximations are stated explicitly.
- Generic textbook summaries that are not used later are avoided.

### 5.3 Criterion C: Critical Thinking

**IB focus (sciences):**  
Analysis should follow accepted scientific processes and address the research question directly. In a secondary-data essay, the analysis must be your own.

**What this means for you:**  
You must do more than describe results. Use graphs, fits, calculations, and comparisons with theory to build an argument that answers the research question.

**Best-practice checklist**

- Graphs are used to test models meaningfully.
- Sample calculations are shown clearly, including uncertainty propagation where relevant.
- Slopes, intercepts, or curvature are linked to physical quantities.
- Anomalies are identified and explained using plausible physics.
- Secondary-data analysis is clearly your own.
- If the data cannot fully answer the question, unresolved issues and concrete improvements are stated explicitly.

> **Highlight**  
> If the reader could remove all your numbers, graphs, and equations and still understand the essay in the same way, then the analysis is not doing enough work.

### 5.4 Criterion D: Discussion and Evaluation

**IB focus (sciences):**  
The discussion should be critical, situated in a scientific context, and always linked to the research question. The conclusion should be a supported synthesis rather than a repetition of results.

**What this means for you:**  
Your discussion must explain what the results mean, how convincing they are, and how limitations affect the strength of your conclusion.

**Best-practice checklist**

- Discussion paragraphs always connect back to the research question and the underlying Physics.
- Sources are used to support or challenge the conclusion.
- The conclusion synthesises the findings instead of repeating every result.
- The evaluation identifies the most important limitations and explains their likely effects.
- Clear distinctions are made between well-supported claims and speculation.

### 5.5 Criterion E: Engagement

**IB focus (sciences):**  
Criterion E is shown through reflection, especially in the RPPF and the viva voce.

**What this means for you:**  
You should show how you personally engaged with the research process: how decisions were made, problems were solved, and the research question evolved.

**Best-practice checklist**

- You describe specific decisions and why they were made.
- You reflect honestly on what did not work and how you responded.
- You connect your experience to bigger ideas about how Physics is done, including modelling, uncertainty, simplification, and iteration.
- You avoid generic statements that do not include real detail.

> **Rubric Link**  
> Use the criteria as a self-check while drafting. After each major section, ask whether an examiner who does not know you or your project could clearly see the evidence for that criterion.
