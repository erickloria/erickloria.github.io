## Purpose

This guide explains how UWC Costa Rica students should cite generative AI in the citation styles most commonly used in school. Because citation guidance for AI changes frequently, students should always check with their teacher and use the most recent version of this document.

## When You Must Cite AI

You must cite AI when it contributes meaningfully to your final work, including when you:

- quote AI output directly;
- closely paraphrase AI output;
- use AI-generated ideas, structure, examples, or wording in your final submission;
- use AI for translation or significant rewriting;
- use AI for translation support in ways that affect your final submitted work (see Section 10.4.4 of the UWC Costa Rica AI Guidelines for translation-specific rules).

You should also describe briefly how you used AI (tool, prompt purpose, and how you verified output).

> **Best practice**  
> When in doubt, cite and briefly explain your AI use. Transparent attribution is safer than omitting a citation.

> **Highlight**  
> **Teacher Override Rule:** Teacher instructions for a specific assignment always take precedence over this guide. If a teacher gives a required format, use that format.

The AI level declared by the teacher for each assignment (see Section 9 of the UWC Costa Rica AI Guidelines) determines whether AI use is permitted and how much citation or disclosure is required. If you are unsure which level applies or what to cite, ask your teacher before submission.

## Style-by-Style Guidance

> **Highlight**  
> Examples in this section use various AI tools for illustration purposes only. For school-related work, only tools approved in Section 4 of the UWC Costa Rica AI Guidelines may be used (currently Gemini and NotebookLM within Google Workspace).

### APA 7th Edition (2025 Update)

This section summarizes current guidance and examples for student use. Always follow teacher requirements for the assignment and assessment context.

#### General Rule

Use APA author-date format. Cite:

- a **specific AI chat** when that exact chat is important for readers to retrieve; or
- the **AI tool generally** when citing a specific chat is unnecessary, unhelpful, or inappropriate.

#### Reference Template: Specific AI Chat

```text
Reference template
AI Company Name. (year, month day). Title of chat [Generative AI chat]. Tool Name/Model. URL of the chat

In-text citation
Parenthetical: (AI Company Name, year)
Narrative: AI Company Name (year)
```

#### Examples: Specific AI Chats

```text
Anthropic. (2025, May 20). Essential grammar topics for high school graduates [Generative AI chat]. Claude Sonnet 4. https://claude.ai/share/329173b2-ec93-4663-ac68-4f65ea4f166d

Google. (2025, May 22). High school grammar concepts overview [Generative AI chat]. Gemini 2.5 Flash. https://g.co/gemini/share/a1306ce12929

OpenAI. (2025, August 21). High school grammar concepts [Generative AI chat]. ChatGPT. https://chatgpt.com/share/68a77b60-0ee4-800c-9acc-cd3fd573c311

Perplexity AI. (2025, May 20). High school grammar topics [Generative AI chat]. Perplexity. https://www.perplexity.ai/search/a457cb8c-c663-4c9b-b34e-cb03d8108b35

In-text examples
(Anthropic, 2025; Google, 2025; OpenAI, 2025; Perplexity AI, 2025)
```

> **Best practice**  
> Model names change frequently. Use the exact model name shown in your own session at the time of use. Examples in this guide may become outdated between updates.

#### Reference Template: AI Tool Generally

```text
Reference template
AI Company Name. (year). Tool Name/Model [Large language model]. URL of the tool

In-text citation
Parenthetical: (AI Company Name, year)
Narrative: AI Company Name (year)
```

#### Examples: AI Tool Generally

```text
Anthropic. (2025). Claude 4 Sonnet [Large language model]. https://claude.ai/new
Google. (2025). Gemini 2.5 Flash [Large language model]. https://gemini.google.com
OpenAI. (2025). ChatGPT [Large language model]. https://chatgpt.com/
Perplexity AI. (2025). Perplexity [Large language model]. https://www.perplexity.ai/
```

#### Prompt Documentation (APA 2025 Guidance)

Prompts are not usually part of the reference itself. If helpful, describe key prompts in your Method section, appendix, or supplemental material, and cite either the specific chat or the AI tool as appropriate.

#### Version Numbers

Do not include version numbers by default unless clearly provided and relevant. Include the model name when available (e.g., Gemini 2.5 Flash, ChatGPT-5).

#### Level 4/5 Disclosure (Integrate/Create)

For assignments declared as Level 4 (Integrate) or Level 5 (Create), teachers may require a task-header or method-note disclosure of AI use instead of (or in addition to) a standard bibliography entry.

```text
APA disclosure example (task header/method note)
AI Use Disclosure: I used Gemini (Google Workspace) to refine structure and language in draft revision (Level 4). All claims and final wording were verified and approved by the author.
```

#### APA Part 2: AI Search and AI in Common Software

```text
APA 2025 quick guidance (Part 2)
1) If AI is used like a search engine to find sources, usually cite the sources you read, not the AI search itself.
2) If AI is built into common software (for example writing-assist features), citation is usually not required.
3) Always verify AI-provided sources and claims before using them.
```

#### APA Part 3: Is AI "Allowed" in APA Style?

```text
APA 2025 quick guidance (Part 3)
APA does not give a universal yes/no rule. AI use depends on instructor, department, and publisher policies.
If AI use is allowed, disclose it transparently, verify all outputs, and retain human responsibility for accuracy, integrity, and final authorship.
```

### MLA 9th Edition

This section summarizes current guidance and examples for student use. Always follow teacher requirements for the assignment and assessment context.

#### General Rule

Use the MLA template and **do not treat the AI tool as the author**. Describe the generated output in the *Title of Source*, list the AI tool as the container, include the specific model in the version element, name the company as publisher, add the generation date, and provide a stable share URL when available (or the tool URL if not).

#### MLA Template (AI Output)

```text
Title of Source. Title of Container, model/version, Publisher, Day Mon. Year, stable share URL (or general tool URL).
```

#### Works Cited Example (Text Prompt)

```text
"Describe the theme of nature in Jane Austen's Mansfield Park" prompt. ChatGPT, model GPT-4o, OpenAI, 23 Sept. 2024, chatgpt.com/share/66f1b0a0-d704-8000-be9a-85f53c850607.
```

#### Works Cited Example (Creative Text)

```text
"The Oak Tree" free verse poem. ChatGPT, model GPT-4o, OpenAI, 23 Sept. 2024, chatgpt.com/share/66f1c740-7500-8000-a38b-6d6045c811f5.
```

#### Figure Caption Example (AI Image)

```text
Fig. 1. "Create an expressionist-style image of two people standing on a beach looking at the ocean" prompt, DALL-E, version 3, OpenAI, 23 Sept. 2024, chatgpt.com/share/66f1c3a3-3f90-8000-9750-82c57c4a6592.
```

#### Secondary Sources and Reliability

If AI provides linked sources, click through, read those sources directly, and cite them rather than citing only the AI summary. AI tools can hallucinate or misrepresent sources.

#### Beyond Citation (MLA Guidance)

When AI use is substantive (for ideation, drafting support, restructuring, or significant revision), add a brief note describing how AI was used so readers can see where AI support ends and your own work begins.

#### Level 4/5 Disclosure (Integrate/Create)

For assignments declared as Level 4 (Integrate) or Level 5 (Create), teachers may require a task-header or method-note disclosure of AI use instead of (or in addition to) a standard works-cited entry.

```text
MLA disclosure example (header/method note)
AI Use Note: Gemini (Google Workspace) was used to propose revisions to paragraph organization and wording (Level 5). Final content decisions and source verification were completed by the student.
```

### Chicago Style

This section summarizes current guidance and examples for student use. Always follow teacher requirements for the assignment and assessment context.

#### General Rule

In Chicago style, credit AI-generated text in your prose or in a note whenever you reproduce it. For many assignments, an acknowledgment in text is sufficient. If a formal citation is required, use a note (or author-date parenthetical) with tool name, provider, date generated, and URL when relevant.

#### Notes-Bibliography Example (Note)

```text
1. Text generated by ChatGPT, OpenAI, [Day Mon. Year], https://chat.openai.com/chat.

1. ChatGPT, response to "Explain how to make pizza dough from common household ingredients," OpenAI, [Day Mon. Year].
```

#### Author-Date Example

```text
(ChatGPT, [Day Mon. Year])
```

#### Bibliography Guidance

Do not include AI chats in a bibliography/reference list unless a publicly accessible link is available (for example, a stable public shared archive URL). Otherwise, treat the exchange like personal communication and document it only in text or notes.

These Chicago examples are illustrative only and use placeholder dates.

#### Editing Disclosure

If AI-generated wording is edited, briefly disclose that (e.g., "edited for style and content").

At UWC Costa Rica, Chicago style is most commonly used in selected humanities and social-science research tasks (for example, some history or interdisciplinary research contexts). If your course does not use Chicago, follow your teacher's required style (often APA or Harvard).

### Other Styles Used in Class

If your class uses another style (e.g., Harvard or subject-specific citation rules), follow your teacher's required format and include the same minimum elements:

- tool/provider name;
- date or version (if available);
- short description of use;
- URL;
- access date (if required by style).

#### Harvard Template Example

```text
Template (Harvard, AI use)
Provider/Tool (Year) Tool or model name. [Description of use]. Available at: URL.

Example
Google (2026) Gemini. [Generative AI used to propose structure and wording options for draft revision]. Available at: https://gemini.google.com.
```

### NotebookLM (Google Workspace)

NotebookLM differs from many other AI tools because it generates responses from sources you upload and select as context, rather than relying only on a general training corpus. This can improve traceability, but it also means you must cite both (1) your NotebookLM interaction and (2) the original source documents used in the notebook.

#### NotebookLM Citation Templates

```text
APA (chat/tool record)
Google. (Year, Month Day). Notebook title or interaction label [NotebookLM interaction]. NotebookLM. URL (if shareable).

MLA (works cited)
"Notebook title or prompt" interaction. NotebookLM, Google, Day Mon. Year, URL (if shareable).

Chicago (note)
Google, NotebookLM response to "Prompt text," [Day Mon. Year], URL (if shareable).
```

> **Best practice**  
> If NotebookLM responses rely on uploaded class readings, articles, or notes, cite those original source documents separately in your bibliography/works cited list. Citing NotebookLM does not replace citation of the underlying sources.

## Academic Integrity Checklist (Before Submission)

Before submitting work:

- confirm whether AI was allowed for the task level;
- verify all AI-supported claims and references;
- clearly mark AI use in citations/notes;
- ensure your final work reflects your own understanding and judgment;
- For consequences of non-compliance, refer to Section 10.4.8 of the UWC Costa Rica AI Guidelines and the UWC Costa Rica Code of Conduct.
