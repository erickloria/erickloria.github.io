Defining Your Research Question

The Physics EE asks a focused, measurable research question grounded in Physics and answered through analyzable evidence.

A strong question should identify a physical system or phenomenon, define variables clearly, and lead to a defensible, evidence-based conclusion.

check_circle Focused: specific enough to be explored deeply within 4,000 words.
check_circle Measurable: built around variables or data that can be defined and analysed.
check_circle Grounded in Physics: linked to accepted models, laws, and scientific reasoning.
check_circle Researchable: supported by primary or secondary data that can be evaluated critically.

Recommended Draft Structure

Title Page & Contents Operational title, exact research question, subject, and word count.
Introduction Phenomenon, why it matters, and why the method or data source fits.
Background / Theory Laws, equations, assumptions, and models needed to interpret the data later.
Variables & Equipment Independent, dependent, controlled variables and instrument uncertainties.
Methodology A replicable procedure or a clear explanation of how datasets were selected and standardized.
Results, Analysis, Evaluation Tables, graphs, uncertainty, interpretation, anomalies, and limitations.
Conclusion & Reflection A supported answer to the research question plus honest engagement with the process.
Bibliography & Appendices Consistent referencing and appendices only for supporting detail, not core analysis.

Word Limit

The EE is a 4,000-word paper. Keep the question narrow enough to stay analytical and manageable.

Assessment and Markbands

rule

How It Is Assessed

The assessment guide separates the EE into formative preparation for the reflections and summative assessment of the essay itself plus the reflective statement on the RPF.

Criteria A, B, C, and D apply to the essay as a whole. Criterion E applies only to the reflective statement.

balance

Best-Fit Marking

Examiners use a best-fit approach. The essay does not need to satisfy every point in a band perfectly to receive marks in that band.

The guide also emphasizes positive marking: credit is awarded for what is present and done well, not by hunting for omissions.

A. Framework for the Essay

6 marks

Guiding question: do the research question, research methods, and structural conventions provide an effective framework for the essay?

  • Research question: relevant, clear, and focused.
  • Research methods: suitable for the question and applied effectively.
  • Structure: conventions support communication of the research.

B. Knowledge and Understanding

6 marks

Guiding question: does the student demonstrate knowledge and understanding of the subject matter being used in the research?

  • Knowledge: relevant research materials establish the subject matter well.
  • Terminology: used accurately and consistently.
  • Concepts: explained and used effectively, not just named.

C. Analysis and Line of Argument

6 marks

Guiding question: does the student analyse the information presented in the essay and produce a coherent line of argument?

  • Analysis should be effective rather than descriptive.
  • Findings should stay relevant to the research question.
  • The line of argument should clearly connect question, findings, and conclusions.

D. Discussion and Evaluation

8 marks

Guiding question: does the student discuss the findings and evaluate the essay?

  • Discussion should balance the significance of the findings with supporting evidence.
  • Evaluation should address the effectiveness of the essay itself.
  • Stronger responses state and explain relevant strengths and limitations.

E. Reflection

Guiding question: does the student evaluate the effect of the extended essay learning experience on them as a learner?

4 marks
Criterion E scope Criterion E applies only to the reflective statement on the student's RPF, not to the essay body.
What the guide asks for Reflection should be evaluative, use specific examples, and show growth, transfer of learning, and how changing perspectives affected decisions.
Use the criteria as a self-check while drafting, but also remember the assessment guide's marking model: examiners award the band that best fits the overall balance of the work rather than requiring every line in a band to be perfect.
