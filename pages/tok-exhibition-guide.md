# TOK Prompt Dissection & Argument Scaffold

*Guide for Pre-Exhibition Planning*

**Last Updated:** March 11, 2026  
**Writer:** Erick Loria Soto  
**Course:** Theory of Knowledge  
**Use:** Planning support for the TOK exhibition

---

## Purpose

Before building a full claim-argument-example structure, first direct and dissect the prompt. This means:

- unpacking the prompt;
- mapping its scope and key terms;
- situating it in relevant Areas of Knowledge (AOKs);
- outlining an initial claim, reasoning, and examples.

Later, you will connect your examples to three chosen exhibition objects and write a focused 950-word commentary.

> **Best Practice**  
> Use precise TOK language such as `evidence`, `justification`, `reliability`, `perspective`, `bias`, `scope`, `certainty`, and `interpretation`. That signals conceptual engagement rather than simple description.

## Directing Your Prompt

When you first meet a TOK prompt, do not jump straight to an answer. Direct it first.

- **Break it down:** Rewrite the prompt in your own words. What is it really asking?
- **Define key terms:** Identify the central terms and give clear definitions, both everyday and TOK-specific.
- **Contextualize it:** Link the prompt to one or two AOKs and the methods or tools used there.
- **Clarify scope:** Does the question apply to all knowledge, or only specific domains? Is it about personal knowledge, shared knowledge, or both?
- **Anticipate tensions:** What debates, disagreements, or conflicts of perspective could emerge?
- **Draft an initial stance:** Write a tentative claim that could guide your argument.

> **Rubric Link**  
> The exhibition must clearly identify three specific objects and their real-world contexts. Avoid vague, symbolic, or generic objects. Make them concrete and situated.

## Building Claim, Argument, and Example

Once the prompt is dissected, start building a scaffolded argument.

- **Claim:** Your direct answer to the prompt in one clear sentence.
- **Argument:** The reasoning that supports your claim, grounded in TOK concepts, AOK methods, and logic.
- **Example:** A concrete case that shows the claim working in context. Later, each example should align with one of your three exhibition objects.

> **Rubric Link**  
> Do more than describe the object. Justify how each object contributes to answering the prompt.

> **Best Practice**  
> Depth beats breadth. Explain what the example shows, why it matters, and how it connects back to the claim.

## Counterclaims and Debate

Strong TOK writing acknowledges alternative perspectives.

- What would a reasonable counterclaim look like?
- How could you respond to it while still defending your claim?
- Does your claim hold universally, or only under certain conditions?

> **Rubric Link**  
> Links between each object and the prompt should be clear, explicit, and convincing.

> **Best Practice**  
> Recognize the strength of the counterclaim before responding to it. That usually makes your final position more credible.

## Reflection and Implications

Strong exhibitions also include reflection and broader implications.

- Reflect on your own position. How do your assumptions or biases shape your view?
- Consider what your argument suggests about knowledge more generally.
- Ask what your analysis reveals about evidence, interpretation, authority, or certainty.

> **Rubric Link**  
> Support your points with evidence and explicit references to the prompt. Evidence can include examples, reasoning, TOK concepts, personal experience, and credible sources.

> **Best Practice**  
> Show self-awareness. Acknowledge an assumption you reconsidered or a perspective that complicated your initial position.

## Preparing for Objects

Later, you will connect your examples to three chosen objects. Each object should:

- clearly illustrate one part of your claim-argument-example structure;
- fit naturally with your chosen AOKs;
- add insight rather than simply restating the claim.

> **Best Practice**  
> Move from `object -> concept -> implication`. The object is not the endpoint; it is evidence for a TOK idea.

## Writing the Exhibition Commentary

Now move from scaffold to finished writing.

**Word Limit:** 950 words maximum. Be concise, precise, and purposeful.

### Suggested Structure

1. **Introduction:** State the prompt, explain why it matters, and introduce your approach.
2. **Body paragraphs:** Present one claim-argument-example sequence at a time, aligned to your objects.
3. **Counterclaim or limitation:** Acknowledge at least one alternative perspective or limit to your argument.
4. **Reflection:** Show moments of self-awareness and explain how your assumptions shape your thinking.
5. **Conclusion:** Summarize your position and highlight broader implications about knowledge.

> **Best Practice**  
> Fewer examples analyzed well are stronger than many examples mentioned superficially.

## Integrating Objects Into the Essay

Each object must be fully explained, not just named.

For each object:

1. **Describe it clearly:** Give its real-world context. What is it? Where does it come from?
2. **Connect it to TOK:** Explain what concept, method, or perspective it illustrates.
3. **Tie it to your argument:** Show how it supports your claim-argument-example structure.
4. **Reflect with it:** Use the object to raise a counterclaim, implication, or personal insight where appropriate.

> **Best Practice**  
> If you could remove the object and your commentary would still make sense, the object is not integrated deeply enough.

## Full Exhibition Rubric

If only **two** objects are chosen, the maximum mark is **6**. If only **one** object is chosen, the maximum mark is **3**.

### Criterion 1: Identification

- **Excellent (9-10):** Clearly identifies three objects and their precise real-world contexts.
- **Good (7-8):** Identifies three objects and their real-world contexts.
- **Satisfactory (5-6):** Objects are identified, but contexts may be vague or imprecise.
- **Basic (3-4):** Objects are identified, but contexts are implied rather than explicit.
- **Rudimentary (1-2):** Objects are generic, unclear, or lack real-world context.

### Criterion 2: Links

- **Excellent (9-10):** Clear and well-explained links between each object and the selected prompt.
- **Good (7-8):** Links are explained but may lack some precision or clarity.
- **Satisfactory (5-6):** Some explanation of links is present.
- **Basic (3-4):** Links are present but unconvincing or unfocused.
- **Rudimentary (1-2):** Links are minimal, weak, or unclear.

### Criterion 3: Justification

- **Excellent (9-10):** Strong justification of the particular contribution each object makes.
- **Good (7-8):** Clear justification of the contribution of each object.
- **Satisfactory (5-6):** Some justification is present.
- **Basic (3-4):** Justification is superficial or repetitive.
- **Rudimentary (1-2):** Very little justification is given.

### Criterion 4: Evidence

- **Excellent (9-10):** Nearly all points are supported by appropriate evidence and explicit reference to the prompt.
- **Good (7-8):** Many points are supported by evidence and relevant references.
- **Satisfactory (5-6):** Some points are supported by evidence.
- **Basic (3-4):** Reasons are offered, but support is weak, inappropriate, or insufficiently relevant.
- **Rudimentary (1-2):** Commentary is mostly descriptive or based on unsupported assertions.

## Final Self-Check

Before submitting, ask:

- Do my three objects have precise real-world contexts?
- Does each object have a clear, well-explained link to the prompt?
- Have I justified how each object contributes to answering the prompt?
- Are my points supported by appropriate evidence and explicit references to the prompt?
- Is the commentary within 950 words and focused on `object -> concept -> implication`?
